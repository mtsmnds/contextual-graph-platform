Concept Note

A Contextual
Reading Platform

A UX concept for turning texts into navigable, relational structures — where books, annotations, translations, and references live side by side and stay connected at the passage level.

The Idea

Starting point

Imagine a site where you're reading a text and it quotes a book. You click that quote, and the full text of the book opens in a column to the right — with the quoted passage already highlighted. From there you can keep reading the book itself, or browse all the quotes drawn from it. Everything stays connected within your page.

A personal workspace

Say I go into my document about The Idiot. It has all my mentions, all my quotes, everything I highlighted, my opinions. If any of those are tied to a specific passage in the book, I click and the full text of the book opens alongside. The navigation shifts — now I'm moving through the book, and my quotes, mentions, and opinions are indexed and aligned to each corresponding part of the text.

The inverse mode

The same system works in reverse. Take a public domain book — I load the full text, and as I read, I can correct it, edit it, connect it to other things. If a paragraph break is missing in this edition, I fix it. That becomes an edit of the text. As I keep reading, I can highlight a passage and leave a comment. All my comments are listed in a column beside the text.

Multi-column, multilingual

This is part of a broader feature: the text alongside multiple columns you can choose from. I want to read The Idiot in English, but I also want a column with the original Russian. The texts are paired by chapter, and I can specify that this passage maps exactly to that one — they stay linked. If I add a note or an idea, I can attach it to a passage and now I have three columns: language A, language B, and my annotations referencing specific parts of the text.

Intertextual expansion

Naturally, this expands. An author in one book cites another work — so you could have both texts present. If The Idiot references Don Quixote, I can have Don Quixote there too. If the reference is to a specific passage, I show a broader context around it. If it's a general reference, it's a link to the work, maybe a summary. If it touches on a relevant theme, I can connect that. The whole point is that the reader has access to expanded context — even the full source — right at the moment things are related.

Concept Validation

What this is, precisely

The concept sits at the intersection of several established paradigms — annotated readers, knowledge graphs, versioned text editors, and parallel-text layouts (sometimes called Talmudic layout or critical editions). Tools like Obsidian, Hypothes.is, Roam Research, and Zotero each touch parts of this space. Academic TEI/XML editions and bilingual corpus viewers do as well. But the key difference here is that the original text stops being static content and becomes a navigable, editable, relational node. That changes everything about the architecture.

Core entities

Four things make the system work:

  • Work — a book, essay, article, or manuscript, with its metadata, authors, languages, chapters, translations, and relations to other works.
  • Segment — the atomic navigable unit. Not a page, not a file — a paragraph, sentence, verse, or logical block. This is the heart of the system: everything references segments, not pages.
  • Annotation — a comment, highlight, insight, critique, link, or thematic tag attached to a specific segment.
  • Relation — a structured semantic connection: "references," "parallels," "contradicts," "echoes thematically," "translates," "comments on."

The UX principle

Progressive contextual expansion. The reader is reading normally. When they click, hover, select, or follow a relation — context expands laterally, never replacing the current view. No invasive modals, no destructive navigation, no loss of reading position. The experience is closer to an IDE, Figma's inspect panel, or Obsidian's graph than to a traditional book reader. Navigation happens on three axes: vertical (linear reading flow through chapters and segments), horizontal (contextual expansion into translations, annotations, references, related works), and relational (semantic jumps between passages across works).

The hardest problem

Stable segment identity. Translations differ in length and structure. OCR editions vary. Edits shift everything. The system must index by logical segment (e.g., book3.chapter2.paragraph14), never by page number or character offset. Anchoring needs to be hybrid — combining segment IDs, text matching, fuzzy anchors, and structural references — so that relations survive when the underlying text changes.

Architecture Direction

Local-first

The first version should be a local-first desktop application — single-user, offline, fast to iterate on. The core problem to solve is UX and data modeling, not infrastructure, auth, or multi-tenancy. Cloud becomes an extension later, not a dependency from the start. A desktop wrapper keeps things lightweight and gives the user full ownership of their data.

MVP scope

The MVP is deliberately narrow: open a book, segment it into paragraphs, create annotations, open a side panel, link passages to each other, save those relations. No AI, no embeddings, no collaboration, no cloud, no social features. If segmented reading plus annotations plus contextual side-by-side relations works well — the core product exists.

What makes it special

This isn't "annotate books." It's transforming reading into structural context navigation — bringing literature closer to the experience of an IDE, a semantic wiki, a research graph, a living academic edition. That space is still largely unexplored in UX.
